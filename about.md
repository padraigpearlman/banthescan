basics:
  label: First, The Basics
  heading: Two technologies, one problem.
  intro: These terms get used interchangeably, but they describe slightly different
    things, and both are part of what we're trying to ban.
  definitions:
  - title: Facial Recognition
    text: Software that scans a camera image of your face, converts it into a mathematical
      map, and compares it against a database of other faces to find a match, used
      by police, landlords, and retailers to identify people without their knowledge
      or consent.
  - title: Biometric Surveillance
    text: The broader practice of tracking people using unique physical traits (face,
      gait, voice, fingerprints) often continuously and at a distance, without the
      person ever being asked to opt in.
case:
  label: The Case Against It
  heading: Four reasons this technology fails the people it claims to protect.
  blocks:
  - stat: 22,000+
    title: Mass Surveillance, Minimal Oversight
    text: The NYPD ran facial recognition searches more than 22,000 times between
      2016 and 2019 alone, largely without public disclosure, warrants, or independent
      oversight of how matches were used or how often they were wrong.
  - stat: 10&ndash;100x
    title: Built-In Bias
    text: 'A landmark 2019 federal study (NIST) found that many commercial facial
      recognition algorithms misidentified Black and Asian faces at 10 to 100 times
      the rate of white faces. That bias doesn''t stay theoretical: it shows up as
      wrongful arrests, disproportionately affecting BIPOC, Muslim, immigrant, and
      LGBTQ+ New Yorkers.'
  - stat: MSG
    title: Corporate Gatekeeping
    text: Madison Square Garden Entertainment has used facial recognition to identify
      and bar attorneys from entering its venues simply because they work for firms
      involved in litigation against the company. Retailers like Wegmans and Rite
      Aid have scanned shoppers' faces without consent, and landlords are rolling
      out facial recognition entry systems in residential buildings.
  - stat: No Opt-Out
    title: Zero Meaningful Consent
    text: Most New Yorkers scanned by these systems never agreed to it and have no
      practical way to opt out, whether they're walking into their own apartment building,
      attending a protest, or just shopping for groceries. That has a chilling effect
      on speech, assembly, and immigrant communities in particular.
