Why Ban?

This technology wasn't built to protect you.

Facial recognition and biometric surveillance sound futuristic. In practice, they're poorly regulated, wrong more often for Black, Asian, and immigrant New Yorkers, and already being used to bar attorneys from arenas, track shoppers, and screen tenants. Here's the full case for banning it.

First, The Basics

Two technologies, one problem.

These terms get used interchangeably, but they describe slightly different things, and both are part of what we're trying to ban.

Facial Recognition

Software that scans a camera image of your face, converts it into a mathematical map, and compares it against a database of other faces to find a match, used by police, landlords, and retailers to identify people without their knowledge or consent.

Biometric Surveillance

The broader practice of tracking people using unique physical traits (face, gait, voice, fingerprints) often continuously and at a distance, without the person ever being asked to opt in.

The Case Against It

Four reasons this technology fails the people it claims to protect.

22,000+

Mass Surveillance, Minimal Oversight

The NYPD ran facial recognition searches more than 22,000 times between 2016 and 2019 alone, largely without public disclosure, warrants, or independent oversight of how matches were used or how often they were wrong.

10–100x

Built-In Bias

A landmark 2019 federal study (NIST) found that many commercial facial recognition algorithms misidentified Black and Asian faces at 10 to 100 times the rate of white faces. That bias doesn't stay theoretical: it shows up as wrongful arrests, disproportionately affecting BIPOC, Muslim, immigrant, and LGBTQ+ New Yorkers.

MSG

Corporate Gatekeeping

Madison Square Garden Entertainment has used facial recognition to identify and bar attorneys from entering its venues simply because they work for firms involved in litigation against the company. Retailers like Wegmans and Rite Aid have scanned shoppers' faces without consent, and landlords are rolling out facial recognition entry systems in residential buildings.

No Opt-Out

Zero Meaningful Consent

Most New Yorkers scanned by these systems never agreed to it and have no practical way to opt out, whether they're walking into their own apartment building, attending a protest, or just shopping for groceries. That has a chilling effect on speech, assembly, and immigrant communities in particular.

Real Stories

This isn't hypothetical. It's already happened here.

These are among the publicly documented cases of Americans wrongfully arrested because of a facial recognition misidentification, exactly the harm these bills are designed to prevent.

Detroit, MI

Robert Williams

The New York Times · June 2020

The first publicly known case of a wrongful arrest based on a facial recognition misidentification. Williams was arrested in front of his family and held for 30 hours after an algorithm falsely matched him to surveillance footage from a store robbery.

It's Already Working

Four places have already banned it. New York should be next.

These bans haven't stopped public safety work. They've just stopped the unaccountable use of biased, invasive technology.

Boston, MA

Banned city government use of facial recognition, including by police, in 2020.
